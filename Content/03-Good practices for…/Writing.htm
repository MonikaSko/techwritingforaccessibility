Writing

This section provides basic information to have in mind while writing accessible technical documentation.

In the beginning, there was a question

Is this reader a new user, a power user, or our prospective user?

and then… more questions

What do they want to achieve? Why are they looking for information?

Is the user in the middle of a task? In a hurry and frustrated? Can the user see properly? How should we facilitate his journey on our page?

Questions are good.

It is good to assess your potential user before creating documentation to help them solve mysteries and potential problems with a product.

The technical documentation you prepare for your users will depend on your target audience, your and their goal, as well as tone of voice.

First, you need to prepare a few things:

  1. Check if the page is well-prepared from the designer and developer's side. Are there any things that are not accessible from their perspective? How can I amend it, and how long will it take?

  2. Make sure you use version control systems (for example, Git). It allows you to track your and others' work. It is a perfect solution to provide users with the latest version of technical documentation without losing track of the previous ones.

  3. Make sure that your docs page has a searchable database. It allows users to search for information quicker and without clicking through the whole page and potentially getting lost and fed up.

If you’ve got your tools and the page for your documentation is prepared, start writing.

When writing accessible documentation, remember to:
  • Use plain language

  • Use the second person when talking to a user

  • Use descriptive links

  • Use clear headers

  • Create a hierarchy on the page

  • Think about information architecture when planning content

  • Make sure your text has a proper size (11pt font) and is correctly aligned (aligned left for EU, Great Britain, and the US)

  • Keep procedural steps clear and simple [ordered lists (bullet or numbered) for step-by-step instructions]

  • Keep your sentences, headlines, and paragraphs short and descriptive

  • Provide context through screenshots, symbols, audio, and videos

  • And always remember about ALT texts

  • Use capitalization and available formatting to help convey meaning

  • Provide a glossary for terms readers may not know

  • Avoid using tables for layout

  • Do not rely on sensory characteristics

  • Create documentation so that it could be accessible on mobile devices

  • Ask yourself often if the language you use would be clear for a 6 yo or your grandma?

Writing for accessibility is also writing for inclusivity, so remember also to:

  • Create gender-neutral pronoun guidelines, and follow them!

  • Do not use discriminatory language

  • Write about your users in an inclusive way

  • Write about your product and its features in an inclusive way
